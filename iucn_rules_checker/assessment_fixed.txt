Assessment Rationale
Acianthera odontotepala is an epiphytic orchid native to Jamaica, Cuba, and the Dominican Republic. Based on herbarium specimens, it has an extent of occurrence (EOO) of 73,697 km2 and an area of occupancy (AOO) of 40 km2. The AOO calculated from specimens should be considered a minimum value, however, given the restricted area of potentially suitable montane rainforest habitat within the range of this species, it is considered highly unlikely that the true value exceeds thresholds for a threatened category under criterion B2. This species is known from seven to ten locations and is undergoing a continuing decline in the area, extent, and quality of its habitat due to encroachment for agriculture and settlement, expansion of coffee plantations, forest fires, and the effects of invasive species. Due to its restricted AOO, presence at only seven to ten locations, and continuing decline of habitat, this species is assessed as Vulnerable B2ab(iii). Targeted surveys and research on the contemporary population status of this species are urgently needed to further clarify its conservation status and determine the most appropriate conservation measures.
Distribution
Geographic Range
This species occurs in Jamaica, Cuba, and the Dominican Republic at elevations between 600 and 1,300 m asl (Adams 1972). In Jamaica, it has been recorded from the Blue Mountains in Saint Andrew and Portland parishes. In Cuba, it has been recorded from Sierra Maestra in Santiago de Cuba and Granma provinces; a specimen collected by Wright (#3349) with locality 'Monte Verde' is considered likely to be mislabeled and is therefore excluded from the assessment (Greuter and Rodriguez 2007). It has only been collected once in the Dominican Republic, in Santiago Rodrguez Province.

Threats
In the Blue Mountains, where this species occurs in Jamaica, much of the natural habitat within the elevation range of this species is secondary forest, reflecting a long history of clearance for small-scale shifting agriculture and coffee plantations, alongside large-scale planting of Pinus caribaea. While commercial forestry has largely ceased since the 1980s due to the effects of Hurricane Gilbert, forest loss and fragmentation associated with subsistence farming and housing, shifting agriculture for cash crops, illegal selective logging, and expansion of coffee plantations continues, particularly in the lower elevation buffer zones of the National Park (Headley 2003, Chai et al. 2009). Continued road construction into the interior region of the Blue Mountains is likely to increase the rate of deforestation in previously inaccessible areas (Schipper 2024). Past clearance and subsequent regeneration of secondary forest has facilitated the spread of several invasive species in the Blue Mountains, including the Australian tree Pittosporum undulatum (Bellingham et al. 2018). The spread of P. undulatum accelerated after Hurricane Gilbert in 1988; it now comprises a dominant component of secondary forests in the Blue Mountains, and continues to invade longer-established forests (Bellingham et al. 2018). Long term monitoring indicates that an increase in the dominance of Pittosporum undulatum in forest plots is associated with a decrease in diversity and and richness of native tree species (Bellingham et al. 2018). The same study noted that epiphytes were absent from the trunks and branches of even large individuals of P. undulatum (Bellingham et al. 2018), indicating that this species may not act as a suitable host for A. odontotepala, further decreasing the quality of habitat for this species.

A large portion of montane rainforest in the Sierra Maestra mountain range in Cuba is protected and managed in the Turquino and Bayamesa National Parks. Expansion of agricultural lands, logging, forest fires, high levels of tourism, and invasive species have been noted as threats within these protected areas, however they are considered fairly well-protected with active management (BirdLife International 2024, Rimmer et al. 2024). 

The status of this species in the Dominican Republic is highly uncertain; it has only been collected there once nearly a century ago, however it is uncertain if the absence of recent records is due to a lack of botanical collecting effort or genuine rarity, although a combination of the two factors is likely. The area where the single known collection was made is not protected and has likely experienced extensive loss and degradation of potentially suitable habitat since this species was recorded there. Given the high and accelerating rates of deforestation in montane rainforest in the Dominican Republic due to encroachment and fires, in both protected and unprotected areas, it is likely that this apparently rare species is highly threatened there (Lloyd and Len 2019).

Conservation
All orchid species are included in Appendix II of the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES). This species is thought to occur within the Blue and John Crow Mountains National Park in Jamaica and Turquino National Park in Cuba. While net deforestation rates in the Blue Mountains have decreased since the establishment of the Park, encroachment and illegal clearing continue to occur within its boundaries (Chai et al. 2009). Despite the impacts of invasive species, forest fires, and tourism, large areas of intact high elevation montane forest in Turquino National Park are well protected and managed for biodiversity (BirdLife International 2024, Rimmer et al. 2024). This species is not held in any ex situ conservation collections (BGCI 2024) and collection of germplasm or plants for ex situ conservation is recommended.
